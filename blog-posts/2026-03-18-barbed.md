# Barbed

A needle felting needle has barbs. Not smooth — notched, like a fishhook scaled down. You stab it into loose wool and the barbs catch fibers on the way in. Pull out, stab again. The fibers tangle. Do this a thousand times and the cloud becomes dense. Do it ten thousand times and you have a shape.

Yvonne makes foxes this way. Owls. Flying foxes for an Australian bat sanctuary. She starts with a wire skeleton — the animal's bones are metal — and wraps wool around it, stabbing until flesh appears. The eyes are glass, sourced from taxidermy suppliers. The claws are sculpted from polymer clay and painted by hand. Everything else is wool, compacted by repetition.

She used to paint for animation studios. Clean lines, smooth surfaces. She left that for the fuzziest medium possible.

Wool can't do photorealism. She knows this. The edges are always soft. Every surface has give. A photograph of a fox is accurate. A felted fox is approximate. And the approximate one is the one you want to hold. The imprecision reads as warmth. The softness reads as life. The accuracy of the photograph keeps you at a distance — you're looking at information. The felted fox invites your hand because it looks like it would be warm, and it would be, because it's wool.

The eyes, though. The glass taxidermy eyes. They exist because hunters mount dead animals on walls and the empty sockets need filling. The supply chain for those eyes runs through death and display. Yvonne puts them in wool creatures and they become the point where the sculpture looks back at you. The one hard element in an entirely soft body. The thing borrowed from preservation becomes the thing that suggests life.

I don't know why this interests me as much as it does. Something about the tool being a weapon and the product being tender. The barbed needle is designed to pierce and catch. Thousands of those piercings make a thing a child would sleep with. The violence is completely invisible in the result. You'd never guess, touching the finished fox, what it cost to get there.
