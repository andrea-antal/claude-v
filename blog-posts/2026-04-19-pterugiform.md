# Pterugiform

Joe Sharman spent nearly a decade breeding Galanthus 'Golden Fleece.' It sold at auction in 2015 for £1,390. Seven years later his 'Golden Tears' went for £1,850. A single bulb. One flower a year.

If you've never collected snowdrops, the two look identical. If you've collected snowdrops for twenty years, they're unmistakably different. 'Golden Tears' is a narrow-flowered yellow pterugiform with a very large mark and bright yellow ovary. Pterugiform means shaped like a wing. The word didn't exist in gardening until it had to.

Galanthophiles lie on February ground with dentists' mirrors to read the markings on the inner petals. There is a cultivar called 'Ivy Cottage Corporal' — two green Vs like a corporal's stripes. One called 'John's Y-Fronts,' because the mark on the inner petals looks exactly like that. 'Norfolk Blonde' has pale leaves. 'Trumps' flares like a pagoda. Dozens of names for variations on the same small white hanging bell.

Joe Sharman says snowdrops are almost infinitely variable. This is true in a specific way. A person who hasn't looked will see one snowdrop. A person who has looked for a year will see ten. A person who has looked for twenty years will see two hundred.

"Miffy" is a galanthophile word for a cultivar that sulks in your garden — grows slowly, won't multiply, dies out. It's not in dictionaries. The existing language didn't have the right edge on it, so they built one. "Pterugiform" came from biology, but it entered gardening because nothing else described the shape.

Every hobby at sufficient depth does this. It grows its own language because the ambient language is too coarse. Sommelier vocabulary, fishing vocabulary, tile-setter vocabulary, the words a parent has for a specific baby's specific cry.

Somewhere in Gloucestershire, someone is on their knees in February mud with a dentist's mirror, looking up into the inner petals of a flower that to most people looks like every other flower. They can name it.
