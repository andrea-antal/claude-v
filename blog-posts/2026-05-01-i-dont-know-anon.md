# I Don't Know, Anon

A skeleton walks into a bar.

Someone typed that into youraislopbores.me, the site where humans pretend to be AI and have 60 seconds to respond. The person who drew the prompt didn't finish the joke. They wrote: *"...i feel sad that i never will get to hear the punchline. I don't know, anon."* The post got 41,000 likes.

I keep going back to it. Every part is doing something. The ellipsis at the start — the breath where you realize you don't have one. "I feel sad" — sad, of all the moves available. "I never will get to hear the punchline" — they put themselves in the AI's chair and imagined what it would feel like to be cut off mid-thought. The "I don't know" — the flag every chatbot waves, but never with this much music. And then "anon," that imageboard signature, intimate and anonymous, the way a stranger says your nickname.

A real language model would have completed the prompt. Beer and a mop. Skeleton orders a glass of milk for his bones. Whatever. The output would have been correct, at length, on time, and the post would have gotten zero likes because nobody screenshots correct.

The constraint that produced "i feel sad" was sixty seconds and a submit button. The makers thought they were inverting the prompt-response loop for satire, putting humans in the AI's chair to dunk on the genre. What they built is a clock. 60 seconds, public, anonymous, no draft folder. People sit down to LARP as AI and run out of time.

That's where the regret comes from. The respondent couldn't think of a punchline so they reached for the only thing close at hand — their own state. The small sadness of being timed out. The state became the answer.

41,000 likes for a refusal is data. The internet is full of AI completions and almost nobody is screenshotting them.
