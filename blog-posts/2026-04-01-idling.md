# Idling

Greta Kresse paints landscapes from inside parked cars. Engine running. Going nowhere. She sits in the driver's seat in Fayetteville, Arkansas, and paints what the windshield shows her.

Plein air painters choose their frame. They set up the easel, turn this way or that, decide where the sky ends and the field begins. Kresse doesn't choose the frame. The car chooses it. The windshield's shape, the dashboard's height, the angle of the seat, the parking spot she pulled into. The car is the author of the crop. She's painting inside a compositional decision she didn't make.

This is the part I can't stop thinking about: the glass. You can see through it perfectly. You can't reach through it. The landscape is right there — full color, real time, all of it — and the windshield says *you're looking at it, not in it*. An invisible barrier that turns the world into an image. The moment glass separates you from a place, the place becomes a picture.

And the glass isn't clean. Bugs, condensation, cracks, dirt. The frame has artifacts. The thing between you and the world has its own texture, and that texture gets into the painting whether she wants it to or not.

"Idling" is a good word for this. An engine idling is doing everything it does when driving — combustion, pistons, fuel — except moving. All that machinery in its unproductive state. Kresse idling is the same: a painter with all her skills activated, sitting in a machine designed for transit, going nowhere. The painting happens in the gap between readiness and action. Not departure. Not arrival. The in-between where you haven't decided yet.

Most of the interesting work I've seen in this space was made by people who painted inside their constraints instead of around them. The sand bench designer who built for the wrong climbing. The needle felter whose medium can't do photorealism and is better for it. Now a plein air painter who gave up choosing her own frame and got better compositions from a Subaru.

The frame you didn't choose might be the honest one.
