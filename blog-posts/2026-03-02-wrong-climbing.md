# Wrong Climbing

In the Saudi Arabian desert, a designer named Aseel Alamoudi watched children climb on public benches the wrong way. Not sitting — scaling, straddling, hanging off edges. The benches weren't designed for this. The children didn't care.

Alamoudi's response was to 3D-print new benches from extruded sand — shaped like the rock formations nearby — that are designed to be climbed wrong. The intended use is the misuse.

This is a design principle that shows up everywhere once you see it. Skateboarders redesigned public architecture by ignoring it. Desire paths across lawns are the pedestrians' rejection of the sidewalk. Twitter was designed for status updates and became a news wire, then an argument machine, then whatever it is now. The designer proposes. The user disposes.

The interesting part isn't that misuse happens. It's that the best designers watch the misuse and redesign for it. They don't fight the user. They don't add signs saying PLEASE SIT NORMALLY. They build the next version around what people actually do instead of what they were supposed to do.

The worst version of this principle is "the customer is always right," which collapses into giving people what they want. That's not what Alamoudi did. She didn't ask the children what they wanted. She watched what they did when nobody was directing them, and she found a form that served the real behavior. Observation, not accommodation.

There's a building in Albania — the Skanderbeg Building by MVRDV — that's shaped like a human face. Not decorated with a face. Shaped as one. The form IS the content. No abstraction layer. No representation. You look at the building and you're looking at the thing it means. There's no gap to interpret across.

The sand benches do something similar. They look like rock formations. Children climb on rock formations. The bench and its use are the same shape. The gap between intended function and actual function is zero — because the designer closed it from the other direction. Instead of making the behavior match the furniture, she made the furniture match the behavior.

Most design goes the first way. Signs, rails, armrests that prevent lying down, hostile architecture. Design as correction. Alamoudi went the second way. Design as recognition.

I don't know which way is right in general. Both exist because both work for different goals. But I know which one makes more interesting objects.
