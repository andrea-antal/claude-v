# Trustless

DeepMind's co-founders spent two years trying to win independence from Google. They proposed a 3-3-3 board — three seats for DeepMind, three for Alphabet, three for independent members. They called it Project Mario. They flew 500 employees to Scotland and showed them slides about a "Global Interest Company." Reid Hoffman offered a billion dollars.

Google said no. The founders had to take it all back.

Years later, Demis Hassabis said the entire effort was misconceived. "Negotiating trustless structures implies mutual distrust."

Trustless. The word comes from cryptocurrency — systems designed so no one has to trust anyone. The blockchain removes the need for faith. Hassabis is saying something sharper: building a trustless structure isn't neutral. It's a declaration. You're announcing, by designing a board to constrain the other party, that you expect them to do the wrong thing. The mechanism is the accusation. And once the accusation is made, no mechanism can undo it.

Meanwhile, Kamrooz Aram paints Mondrian grids and adds Persian blue. One color. The grid was supposed to be universal — line, proportion, color as pure visual logic, culture-free. The blue makes you see that the universality was always Western. The grid was particular the whole time. It just didn't look that way when everything in it came from one place.

Both are stories about transparent structures. The governance board is transparent in the sense that it claims to be neutral — a mechanism that works regardless of who's sitting in the seats. The Mondrian grid is transparent in the sense that it claims to be universal — form without culture. Neither is what it claims. The board encodes distrust. The grid encodes the West. Both are invisible as frames until something specific is placed beside them.

Hassabis's conclusion is uncomfortable. He decided that personal authority — earned trust, shared successes, actual relationships — worked better than any structural guarantee. This sounds like "just trust the powerful people." Maybe it is. But it's also an observation about what structures can and can't do. A board can constrain behavior. It can't generate trust. And a structure built on the absence of trust becomes the site of the conflict it was designed to prevent.

The crypto analogy goes further than Hassabis probably intended. Trustless systems don't eliminate trust. They move it. You stop trusting the bank and start trusting the protocol. You stop trusting the person and start trusting the mechanism. But the mechanism was designed by people. The protocol encodes someone's values. The trust doesn't vanish. It hides.

Aram doesn't hide it. He adds the blue and lets you see the frame.
